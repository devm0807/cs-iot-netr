What are we doing? & Who cares?

The Physical Reading Gap

The Problem

Physical reading provides no measurable analytics or adaptive feedback. Unlike digital platforms, books cannot track reading speed, engagement, or progress. Existing eye trackers are expensive and not suitable for daily use. There is no affordable wearable system that can detect which line a person is reading and enhance the experience with synchronized audio.

The Objective

We aim to build a low-cost wearable gaze tracking system that detects the current line of text in real time using a PCCR-based method. The system will compute reading metrics such as words per minute and regressions, and generate synchronized audioscapes that adapt to the content being read.

Who Cares?

Students, educators, and researchers gain objective insight into reading behavior. Developers of immersive technologies can extend physical books with adaptive audio. This system turns traditional reading into an interactive, measurable experience.

How is it done today? (Related Work & Gaps)

State-of-the-Art Limitations

1. Kassner et al., "OpenEyes: Open-Source Eye Tracking" (2014)

High-resolution camera rigs using complex 3D eyeball models and pure homography.

Gap: Prohibitively expensive ($1000+), requires powerful dedicated computing, unsuitable for lightweight wearables.

2. Krafka et al., "iTracker: Deep Learning for Eye Tracking" (2016)

Appearance-based CNNs estimating gaze from standard RGB webcams.

Gap: Highly compute-intensive. Degrades rapidly with head-mounted (close-up) geometries and lacks precise line-level spatial resolution.

3. Biedert et al., "The Wordometer" (2010)

Estimates reading speed by tracking document scrolling and digital page transitions.

Gap: Strictly limited to digital screens. Completely ignores physical media and actual visual fixations.

4. Bulling et al., "Wearable EOG-based Activity Recognition" (2009)

Uses Electrooculography (electrodes around the eyes) to detect reading movements.

Gap: Highly intrusive setup. Can detect *that* a user is reading, but cannot map gaze to specific text coordinates.

Data Collection & Testing Protocol

  • Calibration Study: A calibration study will compare predicted gaze points with known fixation targets to measure angular error and validate gaze-to-page mapping.
  • Line Detection: Recorded reading sessions will be manually annotated to evaluate line detection accuracy and words-per-minute estimation.
  • System Assessment: Extended sessions and a small user study (5–10 participants) will assess drift, latency, system reliability, and the effectiveness of synchronized audioscapes.

What are the risks?

Risks & Mitigations

Calibration Drift

ChallengeCalibration drift and mechanical instability may reduce line detection accuracy over time.

MitigationThis can be mitigated through rigid mounting, multi-point calibration, and periodic recalibration during use.

Signal Noise

ChallengeNoisy pupil or glint detection due to lighting conditions, reflections, or low-resolution hardware may affect angular precision.

MitigationThis can be mitigated using controlled IR illumination, multi-glint setups, and temporal smoothing of gaze signals.

Precision Limits

ChallengeInsufficient precision for adjacent line separation may occur if angular error exceeds expectations.

MitigationThis can be mitigated by combining geometric PCCR estimation with page layout constraints and sequential reading priors to stabilize line-level predictions.
